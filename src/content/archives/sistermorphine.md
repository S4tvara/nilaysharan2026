title: SisterMorphine
date: 2026-03-15

Some nights arrive quietly.

No wind, no movement, just a strange stillness settling over the world. The streets empty themselves early and the lamps burn with that dull orange glow that makes everything look older than it really is. Time slows in places like that. Not dramatically just enough for a person to notice the small things.

The sound of a train passing somewhere far away.  
Dust drifting through the cone of a broken streetlight.  
The faint hum of electricity running through tired wires.

There are moments when the mind reaches for something softer than reality. Not escape exactly — more like relief. A pause. A quiet place between thoughts where the weight of things briefly loosens its grip.

That is where Sister Morphine lives.

Not in hospitals or medicine cabinets, but in the quiet corners of the mind where people retreat when the world grows too loud. She is not loud or dramatic. She arrives gently, almost kindly, like a familiar presence that asks nothing and promises very little only stillness.

Writers find her when the words refuse to come.  
Soldiers meet her in the silence after battle.  
Lonely men meet her in empty rooms at two in the morning.

She doesn’t fix anything. She never claims to.

What she offers instead is distance the temporary mercy of stepping outside the noise of one's own head. For a brief moment the questions stop pressing. The past loosens its grip. The future waits quietly outside the door.

And in that space, even pain becomes quieter.

But she is not a solution.  
She was never meant to be.

By morning, she is gone.

The trains start running again.  
The streets fill with movement.  
The dust settles back onto the road.

And life continues as it always does.
