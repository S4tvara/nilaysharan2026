## Chapter 1 : An Old Friend

The house stood on the crest of a hill where the morning sun arrived before the rest of the valley. I reached it on a bicycle, a quaint little ride through roads still wet with dew and hidden beneath old leaves.

Beyond an iron gate sat a garden that seemed older than the house itself. An apple tree leaned over a wooden swing, moving slightly in the wind.

An old friend greeted me when the gates opened, and the house rose behind him—a three-story building of weathered stone, with verandas wrapping around a courtyard.

The garden was filled with the sickly smell of old apples. I followed him along a path that curved between overgrown hedges and flowerbeds that had long since escaped their borders. Somewhere above us, hidden among the branches, birds sang without pause, and the house watched in silence.

We stepped onto the veranda and entered through a pair of tall wooden doors. The air inside was cool and carried the faint scent of old paper and polished wood.

At the center of the house lay an open courtyard. Sunlight poured through the gap above, falling across the trunk of the great tree that grew there. Wooden staircases climbed around it in slow spirals, connecting balconies that overlooked the space from every floor.

My friend led the way upward.

Neither of us spoke. Our footsteps echoed softly against the timber steps as we climbed.

On the second floor, he stopped before a door and pushed it open.

The door opened into a forgotten drawing room. Antique clocks lined the walls. Glass cabinets displayed objects whose purpose had been lost to time. Oil paintings hung in heavy frames, their subjects obscured by age and shadow.

At the center stood a long wooden table, large enough to seat twenty people. We sat at the table, and a glass appeared before me. Amber liquid caught the light from the window. We raised our glasses and took a drink.

For a while, it was just the two of us.

Then the guests began arriving.

Some entered through the door. Others seemed to already be there whenever I looked away. The room slowly filled with voices, laughter, and the clinking of glasses.

A woman took the seat beside me.

She sat as if she had always been there, as if this moment had happened many times before.
She and her friends began discussing plans for tomorrow.

I listened carefully.
The details escaped me almost immediately, yet every sentence carried the feeling of something important.
I leaned closer.

The woman laughed at something.

Someone raised a glass.

A chair scraped against the floor.

Then the shot came quiet, almost gentle, like a branch snapping in the distance.

A dull warmth spread through my chest. The glass slipped from my fingers. Amber liquid pooled slowly across the wood.

The woman leaned in, her breath steady against my ear as the room began to tilt and soften.

Her hand rested on the table beside mine, the tips of her fingers stained amber where the spilled liquor had touched her skin.

I looked around the room.

For the first time, I truly saw the faces around me.

The man raising his glass. The woman laughing by the window. The old gentleman asleep in his chair. Each of them felt strangely familiar, as though I had known them once and forgotten them slowly over many years.

Recognition hovered just beyond reach.

The woman smiled.

“You still don’t remember, do you?” she whispered.

Around us, the conversation carried on as before, but now every voice sounded like an old memory returning.

“Why do we keep coming back?”

## Chapter 2 : The Hallway

When I opened my eyes, the drawing room was gone. The pain in my chest had disappeared, but I could still feel the warmth of blood clinging to my clothes. It was soaking through the fabric, heavy and wet against my skin.

The hallway stretched endlessly before us. Doors lined both sides, identical in shape and color, disappearing toward a vanishing point lost somewhere beyond sight. From behind each door came a faint humming. Some sounded like conversations, others like music. A few sounded suspiciously like laughter.

The smell of apples had returned. It was the same sickly, overripe scent from the garden outside, but heavier now. It filled the narrow corridor until every breath tasted like fermenting fruit.

The woman was already walking several paces ahead of me. She looked familiar. Not because I recognized her, but because some part of me wanted to. She had a face assembled from half-remembered nightss and unfinished wishes, the kind of face that always seems to linger just beyond the edge of recollection.She didn't look back as she spoke.

"You spent whole summers under that apple tree," she said.

Her voice drifted effortlessly through the hallway, and the humming behind the doors quieted.

"You thought the beginning of a story was the same thing as its destination." I tried to answer, but my throat felt dry, coated in the dust of the hallway.

A door somewhere to my left clicked open. I caught a sudden burst of distant laughter, but when I turned, the frame was solid wood again.

"You looked for me in crowded train stations," she continued, her pace steady and unhurried. "You gathered pieces of a dozen different people a smile here, a familiar kindness there and convinced yourself they belonged together."

My legs felt heavier with every step. The hallway seemed determined to grow longer whenever I looked away."You called it devotion," she whispered. I stumbled, my shoulder striking the wall. A splinter buried itself deep into my palm, but no new blood came. Still, I dragged myself forward.

The humming behind the doors was becoming deafening. Thousands of voices seemed to speak at once, overlapping until the noise rose and fell like distant waves.

The woman stopped. When she turned completely toward me, the blur of her face seemed to shift. For a brief second, I saw them all the girl beneath the summer sun, a stranger on a train, friends who had slowly disappeared over the years. They were all there, stitched into the same person.

She knelt beside me on the floorboards, and a sudden silence rushed into the space. Her hand reached out, brushing the damp hair from my forehead. Her fingers were surprisingly cold.

"You keep looking for me," she said softly. "But you always look too far ahead." The humming behind the doors faded entirely. The walls began to soften, the solid wood bleeding out into the darkness surrounding us.

"Your destination does not lie here," she said, her voice receding into the shadow. "All I can offer you is a place of refuge tonight. Tomorrow, you must continue."

The darkness swallowed everything.
